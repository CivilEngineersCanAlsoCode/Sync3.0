## 06. AI / ML Depth (When Relevant)

If you mention AI, expect drilling.

• Why unsupervised vs supervised?  
• How was clustering validated?  
• How did you prevent hallucination?  
• What was precision vs recall trade-off?  
• What was fallback if model failed?  
• How often retrained?  
• Any bias risks?

Never say: “Data team handled it.”

**Why unsupervised vs supervised?**  
We chose unsupervised clustering because the primary value was discovery and speed in a domain where labels were either unavailable or unstable. Driver support issues evolve constantly due to policy changes, regional events, product updates, and operational shifts, so a supervised classifier would either require heavy labeling work up front or would lock us into yesterday’s categories and drift quickly. Unsupervised methods let us detect emerging patterns and unknown themes earlier, and then we layered a structured L1/L2/L3 taxonomy on top so the insights were interpretable and actionable rather than just “clusters.”

**How was clustering validated?**  
We validated clustering with a combination of quantitative coherence checks and human ground-truth review loops. Quantitatively, we monitored whether clusters were compact and semantically consistent and tracked drift signals like growth in “misc/noise” clusters or frequent cluster fragmentation. Qualitatively, we ran weekly sampling reviews where ops analysts inspected top clusters, rated whether they represented a single coherent issue, and flagged merges/splits that didn’t make business sense. We also used retrospective incident replays: we took known past incidents and checked whether the system would have clustered complaints in a way that matched what the organization eventually concluded as the root cause, which helped tune thresholds and clustering parameters.

**How did you prevent hallucination?**  
We treated GenAI as an evidence-grounded summarization layer, not a free-form reasoning engine. Summaries were generated from retrieved cluster content and were structured into fixed fields like theme, likely root cause, representative evidence snippets, impact estimate, confidence, and suggested owner, which reduces creative drift. We used confidence gating so low-confidence summaries weren’t pushed to executives or auto-routed, and we had an extractive fallback mode where the system surfaces representative complaints and frequency stats without generative interpretation. The goal was that every claim in the summary could be traced back to underlying evidence, making it defensible in enterprise settings.

**What was precision vs recall trade-off?**  
We optimized recall early in the pipeline and precision at the point of action. For detection, we would rather surface a possible emerging issue than miss it, because early awareness is valuable even if it requires triage. But for executive summaries and routing, false positives are extremely costly because they destroy trust and create organizational noise, so we enforced higher precision via confidence thresholds, minimum cluster size, evidence requirements, and in some cases manual review for high-risk items. This separation—recall for detection, precision for action—made the system both useful and safe.

**What was fallback if model failed?**  
We designed graceful degradation so the product still works when models are uncertain or unavailable. If clustering confidence dropped, issues moved to a manual triage queue with suggested themes and evidence instead of auto-routing. If the generative summarization layer was disabled or unreliable, we produced extractive briefs: top clusters by volume, representative complaints, and trend deltas, which still supported end-of-day reporting. And if the pipeline lagged during spikes, we fell back to simpler trend and threshold-based detection so operational visibility didn’t disappear even if the “smart” layer was temporarily constrained.

**How often retrained?**  
Retraining and tuning were driven by drift rather than a fixed calendar. We monitored signals like decreasing cluster coherence, rising override/edit rates by analysts, vocabulary shifts, and growth in misc clusters. When drift crossed thresholds, we refreshed embeddings, adjusted clustering parameters and similarity thresholds, and updated taxonomy mappings and templates. In parallel, we maintained periodic refreshes aligned to operational cycles, but the key point is that the system had explicit drift monitoring so it stayed accurate as issue patterns evolved.

**Any bias risks?**  
Yes, bias risks are significant because data sources are not representative. Public escalations have loud-minority and language/region skew, which can inflate perceived severity. Channel bias matters too: calls, chats, and social posts represent different user behaviors, so raw volume can mislead. Another bias risk is “volume equals importance,” where frequent minor issues can crowd out rare but critical issues like safety or compliance. We mitigated this by normalizing signals by region and channel baselines, treating public signals as complementary rather than definitive, using severity scoring that accounts for novelty and escalation velocity rather than volume alone, and ensuring high-severity categories received attention even when low frequency.
