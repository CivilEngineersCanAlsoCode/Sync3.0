## 01. Problem Definition (Clarity Test)

If you can’t answer this sharply, you fail. 

• What exact problem were you solving?  
• For whom specifically?  
• What was the pain intensity? (quantified)  
• What happens if this problem remains unsolved?  
• Why was this urgent?  
• Was this must-have or nice-to-have? Why?

You must articulate:

Problem → Impact → Business Risk → Urgency

**What exact problem were you solving?**  
I was solving the problem that Walmart’s driver support ecosystem could not identify and converge on the root cause of recurring driver issues fast enough because signals were fragmented across internal support channels (calls, chats, tickets) and external public escalations (Reddit/X). The organization lacked a unified, systematic way to aggregate signals, cluster semantically similar complaints into root-cause themes, quantify ecosystem impact, and produce an executive-ready summary that could be acted on the same day. The result was that issues kept repeating while teams were still debating what was actually happening and who owned the fix.

**For whom specifically?**  
The primary users were the support intelligence / operations analysts and support leadership responsible for daily and weekly issue reviews—people whose job is to answer “what’s breaking, why, how big, and who should act.” Secondary users were the internal owner teams (ops, product, engineering, regional teams) who needed clear routing and evidence to take action. The economic buyer was leadership responsible for driver operations and support transformation budgets who cared about operational reliability, reputational risk, and cost-to-serve.

**What was the pain intensity? (quantified)**  
Baseline root-cause identification took about seven days end-to-end for recurring issues, largely because investigation and alignment were manual and siloed. A seven-day RCA loop is not just a reporting delay—it means the same issue can generate repeat contacts for a full week, escalate publicly before the organization has clarity, and trigger leadership fire drills. In other words, the pain intensity was “weekly cadence RCA” in a system that needed “daily cadence decisions.”

**What happens if this problem remains unsolved?**  
If the problem remains unsolved, recurring driver issues continue to repeat without containment, contacts increase across channels, and unresolved experiences spill into public platforms, amplifying reputational damage. Support costs rise due to repeat contacts and escalations, teams burn time in alignment meetings instead of executing fixes, and driver trust degrades, increasing churn risk. Over time, this becomes a compounding loop: slow RCA creates more volume, more volume creates more noise, and more noise makes RCA even slower.

**Why was this urgent?**  
It was urgent because the cost of delay compounds daily. Drivers are a supply-side engine—if driver experience is unstable, it impacts reliability and throughput, not just sentiment. Public escalations accelerated urgency because once issues go viral externally, the organization is forced into reactive crisis mode. Leadership needed end-of-day clarity to prevent issues from snowballing, but the existing process produced clarity on a weekly timeline.

**Was this must-have or nice-to-have? Why?**  
This was a must-have because it directly addressed operational stability and risk management, not a cosmetic analytics improvement. A dashboard that shows trends is nice-to-have; a system that reduces time-to-clarity from a week to same-day and routes ownership is must-have because it changes the operating model from reactive firefighting to proactive containment. In enterprise contexts, faster RCA and clear accountability translate into lower cost-to-serve, fewer public escalations, and better retention outcomes—those are core business outcomes, not optional enhancements.

**Problem → Impact → Business Risk → Urgency**  
The problem was fragmented signals and slow manual RCA across internal and public channels, which caused week-long delays in root-cause convergence and ownership assignment. The impact was repeated driver pain and escalating contact volume while teams aligned. The business risk was increased support cost, public reputational damage, and driver churn risk. The urgency came from compounding daily cost and the fact that public escalations can force reactive leadership responses before the organization has clarity.
