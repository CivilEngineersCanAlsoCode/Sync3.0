**Biggest pushback?**  
“The biggest pushback I faced was around standardization. Some stakeholders felt that enterprise customers want fully custom setups, so templates and automation may not work. Another pushback was internal: consultants worried that automation would reduce their role, and they were concerned about losing control over the onboarding quality. In short, the conflict was: ‘Do we keep it high-touch and custom’ versus ‘Do we make it self-serve and scalable.’”

**Engineering constraint?**  
“From engineering side, the main constraint was reliability and scope. Auto-creating topics, themes, dashboards, and personas sounds simple, but at enterprise scale, one wrong setup can create noisy data and break trust. Engineering also pushed back on doing everything in one release—AI topic creation, templates, action layer, persona changes—because it can become too big and risky. So I had to break it down into a safe, phased plan with clear MVP boundaries.”

**Sales overpromise?**  
“Yes, there was pressure from sales and account teams to promise faster onboarding to customers, and sometimes they want to say ‘this is fully automated’ even when enterprise customers still need review and customization. I handled this by setting clear positioning: ‘We can get you to a strong default in days, and customization can follow.’ I also worked with them to define what we can confidently commit to—like standard templates + 30-day quick backfill + persona setups—and what remains optional or paid.”

**Consultant resistance?**  
“Yes, there was real consultant resistance, not because they were negative people, but because this changed how they work. Earlier, consultants owned setup and customization, and their value was visible. With automation, part of that work becomes productized, so naturally they worry about relevance and quality control. I addressed it by reframing the value: ‘We are removing repetitive setup work so you can focus on higher-value consulting—strategy, custom reporting, change management, and stakeholder alignment.’ I also involved senior consultants early so they felt ownership, not replacement.”

**Political pressure?**  
“The political pressure was mainly internal alignment pressure. Different teams had different incentives: services teams measured on implementations, sales teams measured on closing, product wants scalability, and engineering wants reliability. Everyone was right in their own context, which creates tension. My job was to create a plan that protects reliability, improves customer outcomes, and still supports business goals.”

**How did you align conflicting incentives?**  
“I aligned incentives by using a simple shared outcome: reduce time-to-insight and reduce dependency on manual work, without sacrificing quality. I set clear boundaries: templates cover the most common use cases and give quick value, but we keep everything editable so consultants and customers can customize. I also made a phased rollout plan: start with a controlled set of industries and use cases, validate quality, then expand. And I made it clear that this is not removing consulting; it is moving consulting effort from repetitive setup to high-value problem solving.”

### A Real Conflict Story (ready to speak)

“In one phase, consultants pushed back strongly on dashboard templating because they felt every client is unique and templates will create wrong reporting. Engineering also worried about automated setup producing noisy data and increasing support load. I handled it by doing two things. First, I used evidence: we analyzed 1500+ existing client dashboards and found that the core structures and use cases repeat heavily across industries. That showed templates can cover a big majority. Second, I designed the solution to be safe: templates were ‘starting points,’ not locked. Users and consultants could edit topics, themes, and dashboards. We also controlled backfill—like providing a limited window first—so the load and risk stayed manageable. This approach reduced fear on both sides: consultants kept flexibility, engineering got safety, and the business got speed-to-value.”
