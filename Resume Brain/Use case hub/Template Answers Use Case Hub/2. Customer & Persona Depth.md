**Who is the primary user?**  
“The primary users were the people who use Insights daily to monitor brand and customer conversations — mainly analysts and managers in social intelligence, brand, marketing, CX, and sometimes risk or comms teams. Analysts were the heavy users who configure topics, explore data, and build reports. Managers were the users who track performance, monitor risks like crises, and drive actions based on insights. Leadership was also a key user group, but they were more of a consumer of insights rather than a builder — they wanted quick summaries, alerts, and scheduled reports.”

**Who is the economic buyer?**  
“The economic buyer was usually a senior leader — for example, a Head/VP in Marketing, Customer Experience, Digital, Communications, or sometimes the Chief Customer Officer type role. They approve the budget, care about ROI, and want the platform to be adopted across teams. Even though analysts use it daily, the budget decision is made by leadership who expects faster time-to-value and clear outcomes like better crisis detection, better brand perception monitoring, and better reporting.”

**What does their day look like before your product?**  
“Before Use Case Hub, the day was slow and messy. Analysts either waited for consultants to configure everything or spent a lot of time building topics by manually adding keywords, hashtags, handles, and then creating themes for sub-categories. They also had to manually build dashboards for each use case. Managers relied on analysts or consultants to get the right reports, so a simple question like ‘what are top issues today?’ could take days. Leadership often got delayed reports, or they didn’t use the tool much because navigating an enterprise UI felt heavy and not designed for them.”

**What KPIs are they measured on?**  
“Analysts are measured on quality and accuracy of insights, coverage of topics, and how quickly they can produce reports for stakeholders. Managers are measured on time-to-response during incidents, quality of monitoring, and making sure teams act on important trends early — especially crisis or negative sentiment. Leadership cares about outcomes: faster awareness of issues, better brand health tracking, better decision making, and proving ROI from the platform — basically time-to-value and impact.”

**What frustrates them?**  
“The biggest frustration was friction and dependency. Users felt that the product was powerful but too hard to get started with. Analysts were frustrated because setup was repetitive and time-consuming. Managers were frustrated because they could not self-serve and had to wait for experts. Leadership was frustrated because they wanted quick, simple insights but the UI and workflow felt overwhelming and not focused on what they need. Another frustration was that insights were available, but taking action was not built into the workflow in a clean way — so insights didn’t always convert into decisions.”

**What tools were they using earlier?**  
“Before Use Case Hub, they were still using Sprinklr Insights, but in a very consultant-led way. Many teams also used spreadsheets, presentations, and manual reporting workflows. Some customers used separate tools for listening and reporting, and then combined results manually. Even inside Sprinklr, they depended on consultants to build topics, dashboards, and persona setups, and that became the ‘real tool’ — consultants, not the product.”

**What political / org constraints do they face?**  
“In enterprise orgs, access and ownership are very political. Analysts usually own configuration, managers want control but don’t want to do setup, and leadership wants output without learning the tool. Also, different teams have different goals — marketing wants brand perception, CX wants customer issues, comms wants crisis monitoring, and leadership wants a single view. Another constraint is permissions: not everyone should be allowed to edit topics and dashboards because wrong configuration can damage reporting. So we had to support role-based permissions and persona-based experiences to reduce chaos and still keep the product usable for each group.”
