**Timeline from idea → launch**  
“From idea to launch, we delivered the first usable version in about **3 months**. The idea came from seeing repeated onboarding friction across clients and internal consulting teams. After aligning with my VP and key stakeholders, I defined the Listen → Learn → Act flow and broke it into deliverable parts. We started with the highest-impact automation pieces first—topic/theme creation and dashboard templates—then added action features like alerts and scheduled reports, and in parallel we built Persona App to simplify the UI for different roles. The key was to ship a strong MVP fast and then iterate.”

**How did you prioritize?**  
“I prioritized based on what blocks time-to-value the most. I asked a simple question: ‘What is the earliest step that prevents customers from seeing value?’ The answer was setup: topics and themes. So Listen came first—automating the core configuration so data starts flowing. Next was Learn—templates so customers can see insights without building dashboards manually. Then Act—alerts and scheduled reports so insights become decisions. Persona App was prioritized alongside because even if we give dashboards, leadership still won’t adopt if the UI stays overwhelming. I also prioritized coverage: common industries and common use cases first, and left edge cases for later.”

**What slipped?**  
“A few things slipped mainly around breadth. Initially, there was a temptation to cover too many industries and too many use cases in the first release. We realized that trying to ship ‘everything’ would reduce quality and increase risk. So we reduced scope and shipped fewer templates with higher confidence, and we planned to expand template coverage in later iterations. Some of the advanced workflows also needed more time to polish for enterprise-grade reliability.”

**Biggest mistake?**  
“My biggest mistake early on was underestimating how sensitive enterprise customers are to trust and data quality. When you auto-generate topics and themes, even small noise feels risky to customers. So I learned that automation needs transparency and control. I corrected this by ensuring every AI-generated output is visible and editable, and by positioning it as ‘a strong starting point’ rather than ‘a perfect final setup.’”

**What went wrong?**  
“What went wrong at times was stakeholder expectations and scope pressure. Sales and internal teams wanted us to promise a fully automated onboarding, while engineering was worried about reliability, and consultants worried about flexibility. Also, technically, backfill and data load needed careful control—if we auto-backfill too much for everyone, it can create performance issues. So the risk was: if we move too fast without guardrails, we could damage trust and platform performance.”

**How did you recover?**  
“I recovered by putting clear guardrails and a phased approach. First, I clarified the product promise: fast default setup + editable controls, not ‘fully automatic and perfect.’ Second, I implemented safety boundaries like limited default backfill and optional paid deeper backfill. Third, I reduced scope and focused on the most common templates that cover most customers, instead of trying to cover every edge case. Fourth, I involved consultants early in template design so they felt ownership and we used real best practices. That combination helped us ship on time with quality and made it easier to iterate after launch.”
