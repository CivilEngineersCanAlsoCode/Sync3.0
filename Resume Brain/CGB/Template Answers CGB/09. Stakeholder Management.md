## 09. Stakeholder Management

No real product launches without friction.

• Biggest pushback?  
• Engineering constraint?  
• Sales overpromise?  
• Consultant resistance?  
• Political pressure?  
• How did you align conflicting incentives?

Give a real conflict story.

---

**Biggest pushback?**  
The biggest pushback came internally from engineering and consulting teams when I proposed stripping down modules from the executive interface. The enterprise mindset equated more features with more value. Removing publishing, configuration panels, and deep drill-down modules felt counterintuitive to stakeholders who had invested heavily in building them. There was a concern that simplifying the interface would reduce perceived product strength.

**Engineering constraint?**  
Engineering initially resisted creating a separate executive-optimized layer because it introduced parallel display logic and additional maintenance complexity. From their perspective, maintaining a single universal dashboard framework was cleaner and more scalable. They were concerned about edge cases and future custom requests if we opened the door to executive-specific views.

**Sales overpromise?**  
Sales had positioned the platform as an all-in-one intelligence suite. There was tension because simplifying the executive interface meant not exposing the full product capability at that level. Sales feared that limiting visible functionality could reduce perceived value during executive demos.

**Consultant resistance?**  
Consultants were the most sensitive stakeholder group. Prior to the redesign, executives relied heavily on consultants to interpret dashboards and prepare summaries. A simplified executive interface reduced that dependency. Some consultants were concerned this would diminish their role and influence in executive sessions.

**Political pressure?**  
Political sensitivity was high because this deployment served the Prime Minister’s Office. Any misclassification, incorrect alert prioritization, or localization mistake could have reputational consequences. That created a risk-averse environment where stakeholders preferred not to change too much. There was also pressure to maintain credibility and avoid visible disruption during rollout.

**How did you align conflicting incentives?**  
The conflict centered on a fundamental question: Is the goal feature exposure or executive decision clarity? I aligned incentives by grounding the discussion in observable evidence. I presented usage analytics showing shallow engagement across complex modules and documented executive session observations where consultants had to summarize insights despite dashboards being present. I reframed simplification not as feature removal but as audience segmentation—analyst workflows would remain intact in the enterprise environment, while executives would receive a clarity-optimized layer.

With consultants, I positioned the redesign as enabling them to focus on higher-value strategic interpretation rather than routine summarization. For engineering, I proposed modular display abstraction so that executive surfaces reused core intelligence logic rather than duplicating backend functionality. For sales, I demonstrated how executive adoption increases contract defensibility and renewal probability, strengthening long-term revenue rather than reducing perceived value.

The turning point came during a prototype demo where executives reacted positively to the simplified hierarchy and explicitly stated that clarity improved. That feedback reduced internal resistance because it validated the hypothesis directly from the end user.

The real conflict story is this: the organization initially valued product breadth; I had to advocate for product subtraction. Alignment happened when we shifted the definition of success from “feature completeness” to “time-to-decision.”
