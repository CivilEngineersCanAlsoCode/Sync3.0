## 02. Customer & Persona Depth

If you don’t understand the user, you didn’t build the product.

• Who is the primary user?  
• Who is the economic buyer?  
• What does their day look like before your product?  
• What KPIs are they measured on?  
• What frustrates them?  
• What tools were they using earlier?  
• What political / org constraints do they face?

---

**Who is the primary user?**  
The primary users were senior executive stakeholders within the Prime Minister’s Office of Qatar and ministry leadership teams. Specifically, this included executive advisors, strategic oversight teams, and ministers responsible for service performance and public-facing accountability. These were not analysts or operators; they were decision-makers who needed prioritized intelligence to determine whether intervention was required. Their interaction time with any system was limited, high-stakes, and outcome-focused.

**Who is the economic buyer?**  
The economic buyer was the digital governance and transformation sponsor within the Prime Minister’s Office who owned modernization initiatives and oversight systems. Budget justification was tied to risk mitigation, governance efficiency, and cross-ministry transparency. The buyer cared about institutional reliability, executive adoption, retention, and long-term strategic value rather than feature-level functionality.

**What does their day look like before your product?**  
Before the product redesign, their day began with consultant-prepared summaries, static reports, and fragmented dashboards. Intelligence was consumed through PowerPoint briefings, PDF exports, and manual issue escalations. To understand public sentiment or complaint spikes, they relied on intermediaries to interpret data. Decision cycles were delayed because signals had to be stitched together from multiple systems. Meetings often focused on reconciling conflicting data rather than taking action. Instead of clarity within minutes, executive review sessions could extend beyond 20–30 minutes just to establish context.

**What KPIs are they measured on?**  
They were measured on public satisfaction stability, response latency to emerging issues, complaint resolution velocity, service performance perception, and reputational integrity. At a broader level, they were accountable for governance credibility and coordinated ministry performance. Their performance indicators were not traditional product metrics but political and operational outcomes: reduced escalations, stable public narrative, and visible responsiveness.

**What frustrates them?**  
Their primary frustration was signal overload without prioritization. They faced too many dashboards with inconsistent hierarchies and lacked a clear “what requires attention now” view. English-biased interfaces created friction in an Arabic-first executive context. They were also frustrated by dependence on consultants to extract insights from tools that were not designed for executive scanning. Cognitive overload and lack of trust in machine-translated sentiment created skepticism toward outputs.

**What tools were they using earlier?**  
They were using the full enterprise Sprinklr suite, which was feature-rich but analyst-heavy. In addition, they relied on internal complaint ticketing systems, helpline logs, survey reports, spreadsheets, and PowerPoint briefings prepared by consultants. Media monitoring and social listening tools existed, but they operated in silos. There was no unified executive intelligence layer synthesizing these sources into prioritized decision surfaces.

**What political / org constraints do they face?**  
They operated within a hierarchical governance structure where data accuracy and tone sensitivity were critical. Misclassification of sentiment or misinterpretation of Arabic nuance could have political consequences. Decision-making visibility was high, and there was low tolerance for ambiguity or inaccuracy. There were also cross-ministry sensitivities, meaning access control and data segregation were critical. Additionally, cultural expectations required formal tone consistency and respect for contextual nuance in executive communication. Adoption depended not only on usability but on trust, credibility, and institutional alignment.
