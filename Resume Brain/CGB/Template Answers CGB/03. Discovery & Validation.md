## 03. Discovery & Validation

This separates real PMs from feature executors.

• How did you validate the problem?  
• What assumptions were wrong?  
• What user research did you do?  
• What surprised you?  
• Why this solution vs alternatives?  
• What evidence gave you conviction?

---

**How did you validate the problem?**  
I validated the problem by triangulating three signals: stakeholder pain articulation, observed behavior, and product usage evidence. On the stakeholder side, PMO and ministry leadership repeatedly described the same failure mode: too many sources, inconsistent narratives, and delayed awareness during emerging issues. On the behavior side, I observed executive review sessions and saw that even when dashboards existed, leaders still asked consultants to “summarize what matters,” which meant the dashboard was not producing decision clarity. On the usage side, we saw underutilization of complex modules, shallow navigation depth, and repeated reliance on a small subset of views. Together, this established that the real gap was not “more data” but “executive-grade prioritization and clarity within minutes.”

**What assumptions were wrong?**  
The biggest wrong assumption was that executives wanted more granularity and more feature coverage. In reality, executives wanted subtraction, not addition. Another assumption that broke was that localization could be handled as a translation layer; we learned that Arabic comprehension, tone, and cultural framing required an Arabic-first design approach, not English-first UI with translated labels. A third incorrect assumption was that a single “master dashboard” would work across ministries; instead, each ministry needed a consistent framework but tailored hierarchy and alert emphasis depending on their services and risk profile.

**What user research did you do?**  
I ran structured stakeholder interviews with PMO executive staff and ministry leadership to understand their decision loops, what triggers escalation, and what “good” looks like in an executive sitting. I conducted workflow mapping of how issues moved from public signal to internal escalation and then to action. I used observational research by sitting through review sessions where dashboards were consumed live, watching where confusion happened and where decisions stalled. I also ran localization feedback sessions with native Arabic consultants to validate tone, intent preservation, and cultural appropriateness, because language errors were not just usability problems—they were trust breakers. Finally, I used product analytics and engagement patterns to validate what users actually touched versus what they said they wanted.

**What surprised you?**  
The most important surprise was that executives didn’t want exploration—they wanted closure. They weren’t trying to investigate; they were trying to answer “Do we need to act?” within minutes. A second surprise was how quickly trust collapses with poor Arabic phrasing; even small tone mismatches made the system feel unprofessional and unreliable, which reduced adoption irrespective of underlying data quality. A third surprise was that the presence of many modules created perceived complexity even if users didn’t open them; the UI footprint itself became a barrier, so simplification had psychological impact beyond functional impact.

**Why this solution vs alternatives?**  
We considered alternatives like training executives on the full enterprise product, relying on consultants as permanent interpreters, or generating static daily reporting. Training would not scale and would fail under time constraints because leaders cannot adopt analyst workflows. Consultant gating created dependency risk and slowed reaction time precisely when speed matters. Static reporting solved visibility but not real-time early-warning detection. The chosen solution—unified intelligence layer plus executive-first surfaces—was the only approach that addressed the core requirement: decision-grade clarity in one sitting, repeatedly, without intermediaries, and without cognitive overload.

**What evidence gave you conviction?**  
Conviction came from converging proof rather than a single signal. Executive observation showed repeated drop-off and consultant reliance, proving that the existing interface failed its primary job. Usage data showed consistent shallow engagement patterns, indicating that complexity was suppressing adoption. Interviews established that “time-to-clarity” was the actual job-to-be-done, not feature richness. Localization reviews demonstrated that translation-only approaches created trust damage, making an Arabic-first redesign non-negotiable. When we prototyped simplified views and validated them with stakeholders, the feedback shifted from “this is complex” to “this is usable,” which confirmed that simplification, prioritization, and language precision were the right levers.
