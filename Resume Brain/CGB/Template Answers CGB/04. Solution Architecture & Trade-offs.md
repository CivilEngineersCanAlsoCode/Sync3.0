## 04. Solution Architecture & Trade-offs

This is where strong PMs stand out.

• Why this architecture?  
• What were 2–3 alternative approaches?  
• What trade-offs did you accept?  
• What did you deliberately NOT build?  
• What was technically risky?  
• What scalability risks existed?

If you can’t articulate trade-offs, interviewer will doubt ownership.

---

**Why this architecture?**  
We chose a unified intelligence layer architecture that aggregated four independent data streams—media monitoring, social listening, survey inputs, and complaint systems—into a centralized processing and prioritization layer, which then powered two surfaces: 21 executive display screens and a simplified Prime Minister mobile interface. The architecture separated ingestion and normalization from presentation. This was intentional. Executives do not need raw feeds; they need structured, prioritized signals. By centralizing intelligence processing and layering executive-first surfaces on top, we ensured consistency across ministries while enabling tailored display logic per stakeholder. This architecture also prevented duplication of logic across dashboards and ensured scalability when new ministries or signal types were added.

**What were 2–3 alternative approaches?**  
The first alternative was to retain the full enterprise Sprinklr product and train executives to navigate it. This would have minimized engineering changes but would have required executives to adopt analyst workflows, which contradicted observed behavior. The second alternative was to create static daily or weekly executive summary reports generated automatically. While simpler, this would not support real-time early-warning detection, which was core to the problem. The third alternative was to rely on consultant-mediated dashboards, where analysts interpreted signals and escalated only major issues. That approach would reduce cognitive load for executives but introduce latency and dependency risk. Each alternative failed to satisfy the requirement of rapid, independent, executive-grade clarity within minutes.

**What trade-offs did you accept?**  
The primary trade-off was depth versus clarity. We sacrificed granular drill-down flexibility in favor of prioritized, glanceable intelligence. We reduced module breadth to improve adoption and trust. We also accepted that some analyst workflows would remain in the enterprise version rather than the executive interface. Another trade-off was recall versus precision in early-warning detection; we prioritized broader signal capture to avoid missing emerging issues, then layered alert prioritization to control noise. We also traded short-term feature completeness for long-term executive usability, meaning some advanced analytics capabilities were deferred.

**What did you deliberately NOT build?**  
We deliberately did not include publishing workflows, campaign management modules, or deep configuration panels within the executive app. We did not build end-to-end operational ticket management inside the PM interface because that would blur the line between oversight and execution. We also avoided building overly customizable dashboards for executives, as customization increases complexity and maintenance overhead. Instead, we standardized hierarchy and risk classification logic to maintain cognitive consistency across ministries.

**What was technically risky?**  
The most technically risky component was real-time ingestion and normalization across heterogeneous data sources, especially during volume spikes caused by crises or media surges. Ensuring low latency while preserving data accuracy required careful coordination between batch and streaming pipelines. Another risk was Arabic sentiment interpretation and entity detection, where misclassification could undermine trust. Executive-facing products have lower tolerance for error, so reliability thresholds were stricter than in analyst environments.

**What scalability risks existed?**  
At 10x scale, ingestion latency and alert noise amplification were the primary risks. Increased ministries or signal types could overload the prioritization logic if not carefully structured. Multi-tenant data segregation was also critical; cross-ministry data exposure would create political and compliance issues. Additionally, as adoption increased, reliance on unified processing meant that a bottleneck in the intelligence layer would affect all executive surfaces simultaneously. We mitigated these risks through centralized normalization logic, role-based access controls, and modular display design so that additional ministries could be onboarded without rewriting the intelligence core.
