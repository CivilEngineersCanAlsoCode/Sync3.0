## 12. UX & Product Thinking

• How did you reduce cognitive load?  
• Why your chosen user journey?  
• What usability metrics did you track?  
• How did you validate design?  
• What did users initially struggle with?

---

**How did you reduce cognitive load?**  
Cognitive load was reduced primarily through subtraction and hierarchy control. I removed non-essential modules from the executive interface, eliminated redundant navigation paths, and reduced visible configuration options. Instead of presenting data categories equally, I introduced prioritization layers with risk-based color coding and structured alert panels. I limited the number of primary decision surfaces visible at one time and standardized layout patterns across ministries to reduce mental switching costs. The interface shifted from exploration-oriented to decision-oriented, ensuring that the first screen answered the highest-probability executive question: “Is there anything critical right now?” Arabic-first redesign also reduced linguistic friction, which directly lowered cognitive strain during scanning.

**Why your chosen user journey?**  
The user journey was intentionally linear and goal-focused: open → scan risk level → review top escalations → exit. Executives do not browse; they verify status. Therefore, the journey minimized branching paths. High-risk items were surfaced immediately without requiring filtering or configuration. Secondary views were available but not visually dominant. The structure mirrored real executive meetings, where the first question is whether intervention is needed. Designing the journey around that mental model reduced navigation friction and time-to-decision.

**What usability metrics did you track?**  
We tracked session duration distribution, navigation depth before reaching priority signals, alert click-through rates, module abandonment rates, and repeat login frequency. We also monitored concentration of engagement across primary panels; ideally, most executive sessions should focus on prioritized sections rather than exploratory tabs. Reduction in consultant-mediated explanation during executive meetings was a qualitative usability indicator. Post-redesign, shorter yet purposeful sessions within the 5–10 minute range indicated improved clarity.

**How did you validate design?**  
Design validation occurred through live observation sessions with executive stakeholders and consultants. Instead of relying solely on feedback surveys, we observed where hesitation occurred, where navigation paused, and which panels drew immediate attention. We also ran iterative prototype demos before full deployment to validate information hierarchy and tone consistency in Arabic. Localization validation involved native consultants reviewing phrasing and visual emphasis to ensure cultural alignment. If executives could interpret a screen correctly without explanation, it passed validation; if explanation was required, redesign was necessary.

**What did users initially struggle with?**  
Users initially struggled with feature overload and unclear prioritization. The presence of multiple modules created decision paralysis even when most were unused. English-centric labeling in earlier versions created friction in an Arabic-first context. Additionally, earlier dashboards presented equal-weight data categories, forcing executives to interpret importance themselves. That cognitive burden led to reliance on consultants. By restructuring the hierarchy and simplifying the visual footprint, we addressed these friction points directly.
