## 02. Customer & Persona Depth

If you don’t understand the user, you didn’t build the product.

• Who is the primary user?  
• Who is the economic buyer?  
• What does their day look like before your product?  
• What KPIs are they measured on?  
• What frustrates them?  
• What tools were they using earlier?  
• What political / org constraints do they face?

**Who is the primary user?**  
The primary user is the support intelligence / operations analyst (and their immediate support leadership) responsible for running the daily and weekly “issue health” process for the driver ecosystem. Their core job is not to read tickets; it’s to convert chaotic, high-volume complaints into a clear narrative: what’s recurring, what’s new, what’s the likely root cause, how big is the impact, and who should own resolution. They sit at the center of the org as the bridge between support noise and cross-team action, and they’re judged on whether they can create clarity early enough to prevent escalation.

**Who is the economic buyer?**  
The economic buyer is leadership responsible for driver operations and driver support transformation—people who own budgets tied to operational reliability, support cost reduction, escalation risk, and driver experience stability. They fund this because it reduces cost-to-serve, prevents public crises, and improves operational cadence. In enterprise reality, the buyer’s bar is: does it reduce risk and improve measurable operational outcomes at scale, with strong governance and trust.

**What does their day look like before your product?**  
Before the product, their day was dominated by manual triage and alignment. They would pull scattered reports from internal systems (calls, chats, tickets), separately check social listening dashboards or ad-hoc alerts for public escalations, and then manually sample conversations to infer patterns. A large chunk of time went into debating scope—whether multiple symptoms were one root cause or several—and into coordinating with multiple teams to get consensus and ownership. Reporting was usually slide/spreadsheet-driven, built late, and biased toward the loudest signals because there was no unified, quantified ecosystem view. The result was that their “analysis” became a mix of detective work and politics rather than a repeatable operational process.

**What KPIs are they measured on?**  
They’re measured on speed to clarity and impact reduction. That shows up as time-to-detect and time-to-root-cause convergence, reduction in repeat-contact rate for the same underlying issue, fewer escalations to public channels, and shorter time-to-resolution once an issue is identified and routed. They also get judged on executive confidence: leadership needs to trust the daily readout and believe that it reflects reality, not anecdotes. In many setups, they’re also indirectly accountable for support efficiency—containing contact volume and preventing issue recurrence from driving cost.

**What frustrates them?**  
Their biggest frustration is not “too much work,” it’s low leverage. They face signal overload without reliable clustering, fragmented truth across internal and public channels, and slow convergence because teams argue about root cause and ownership. They also fear being wrong: a false escalation or incorrect root cause in front of leadership can damage credibility, so they become conservative and slow, which makes outcomes worse. Finally, they get stuck in a reactive loop—issues get attention only after they’re loud—so their work feels like firefighting instead of prevention.

**What tools were they using earlier?**  
They were using a patchwork: internal ticketing/CRM and call/chat logs for support data, social listening dashboards for public escalations, and then spreadsheets and slide decks to manually synthesize findings and present to leadership. Detection was often keyword-based and brittle, and correlation between internal spikes and external escalation was largely manual. Even when dashboards existed, they served visibility, not root-cause convergence or ownership routing, which is why the process stayed slow.

**What political / org constraints do they face?**  
They operate inside heavy cross-team politics because systemic issues cut across support, ops, product, and engineering. Ownership is often ambiguous, and routing issues can feel like blame assignment, so teams resist accountability or bounce cases. Incentives are misaligned: support is incentivized to close tickets fast, ops to maintain throughput, product to protect roadmap priorities, and leadership to avoid public incidents—so even good insights can stall without governance. Data access is also constrained by privacy and compliance; support logs can contain PII, and linking public escalations with internal profiles raises sensitivity. Finally, enterprise stakeholders demand trust: explainability, auditability, and low false positives, otherwise adoption dies quickly.
